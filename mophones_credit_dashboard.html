⚠️ Why the 6–12mo Band Matters Operationally

PAR30 jumps from 13.6% (3–6mo) to 26.5% (6–12mo) — a near-doubling in a single cohort step. This is the critical intervention window. By the time loans reach 1–2yr, write-off rates hit 29.6%, arrears average KES 34,516, and accounts are no longer classified as 'Active' — collections options narrow significantly. The 6–12mo band is where proactive restructuring or targeted outreach has the highest chance of reversing trajectory before the account hardens.

📌 Operational Implication

  • Set automated 90-day check-in triggers at loan age day 150 (mid 5th month) — before accounts cross into the high-risk band
  • FPD rate is highest in 0–3mo (7%) and 1–2yr (6.5%) — different root causes requiring different interventions
  • The growing portfolio means the 6–12mo cohort will swell in 2026 — if not actioned, PAR30 headroom will narrow further
  • Collection rate collapses from 93% at <7 DPD to 29.7% at 365+ DPD — speed of intervention is the single biggest cost lever

⚡ The Core Tension: Collections Effectiveness vs. Customer Experience

🔴 Collections Pressure Creates CX Damage

Device blocking (729 accounts at avg 57 DPD) is an aggressive enforcement tool. For customers who have made partial payments or experienced payment delays not of their own making — such as mobile money transmission lags — blocking creates outrage and dispute volume. Returns are 72% out-of-policy, suggesting customers are abandoning agreements and returning devices as a last resort. This is not just a credit event; it is a service breakdown signal.

🟢 Early-Stage Customers Are Well-Served

The 0–3 month cohort has a 99%+ collection rate and 70.5% active rate — new customers are performing well and presumably having a satisfactory experience. The FPD rate has improved year-on-year (6.8% → 5.5%), suggesting better onboarding or credit screening. The tension only emerges at 6+ months when payment fatigue sets in and device lock enforcement kicks in.

💡 Concrete Recommendation: Introduce a "Payment Grace Window" Protocol with Proactive Outreach

Currently, device locking appears to trigger at or around 31–60 DPD (BLOCKED 31-60 status). For accounts that have a history of on-time payments and slip for the first time — implement a 7-day grace window + automated outreach (SMS/push via MoApp) before blocking. Pair this with a lightweight self-service restructuring option accessible via the app: allow a one-time payment plan revision without agent intervention.

The data shows collection rate is still 84.4% at 8–30 DPD — proactive contact in this window is highly recoverable. Once blocked (31–90 DPD), rate drops to 69.8%. Preserving the customer relationship at that 30-day threshold is both a CX win and a credit recovery win. Segment the grace window eligibility by client model and loan age — 1C customers with a good first 5 months get the grace period; repeat FPD accounts do not.

🚩 Limitations Encountered

1. CUSTOMER_AGE is mislabelled — it is loan age in days, not the borrower's age

Values range from 1 to 1,056 days, which is inconsistent with any plausible human age. The schema also lists AGE_BAND as a separate field (not present in files), which further suggests customer demographic age was intended but never implemented. This means no demographic age-based risk segmentation is currently possible, despite it being signalled in the schema. A borrower aged 22 may have very different risk behaviour from one aged 45 — this is a fundamental blind spot.

2. Income data is almost entirely unlinked (98.9% null Loan Id in Income Level sheet)

The Income Level sheet in Sales_and_Customer_Data.xlsx contains 22,839 rows of income data (monthly received, bank flows, paybill inflows) but only ~260 rows have a Loan Id populated. This means income-based risk segmentation — one of the most powerful predictors of credit performance — is entirely unavailable. The data exists but isn't joined. This is a pipeline/ETL gap, not a collection gap.

3. NPS survey file not present despite being referenced in the brief

The brief (and original schema) references a full NPS dataset with 17 columns including satisfaction scores, qualitative feedback, and device/support experience. This file was not uploaded. Q2 was answered using credit and return data as proxies, but the richest signal for understanding collections-experience tension — verbatim customer feedback — was inaccessible. This is a data delivery gap that should be closed for any future CX-credit analysis.

4. Inconsistent schema across snapshots (Unnamed: 28 ghost column, variable column counts)

Jan and Mar snapshots have 33 columns; Jun, Sep, Dec have 34 — with the extra column being Unnamed: 28, which is 100% null across all three files. This indicates a broken export process that introduced a phantom column from row 30 Sep onwards. It signals that the data pipeline has no schema validation step — columns can silently appear or disappear between snapshots, making automated monitoring brittle.

✅ Recommended Improvements

Fix 1 · Rename CUSTOMER_AGE → LOAN_AGE_DAYS and add BORROWER_AGE as a true demographic field

Immediately rename the field in all schemas and exports to eliminate confusion. Add a true BORROWER_AGE (or link to the DOB sheet which exists in the Excel workbook) as a standard credit field. This unlocks demographic risk segmentation — a core underwriting and regulatory compliance requirement. The DOB sheet already has date_of_birth and Loan Id — building this linkage is a one-sprint task.

Fix 2 · Enforce Loan Id as a mandatory FK in all support tables (Income, Gender, DOB)

The Income Level and Gender sheets exist but are 99%+ unlinked due to missing Loan Id values. Implement a database-level NOT NULL constraint on Loan Id in these tables, and backfill using the existing SALE_ID / Loan Id mapping in Sales Details. Once linked, income-based segmentation becomes immediately available — median income in the Income Level sheet is KES 324,000/month, which could power meaningful credit tiering if properly joined.

Fix 3 · Implement schema versioning and a column-count validation check on every snapshot export

The ghost Unnamed: 28 column and the missing fields (IS_DELINQUENT, AVG_MONTHLY_INCOME, DURATION_MONTHS) indicate no automated validation exists at export time. Introduce a schema manifest (a simple JSON file listing expected columns, types, and nullable flags) and a CI check that compares each new export against the manifest before it is distributed. Flag any diff — added columns, removed columns, or type changes — for human review. This prevents silent schema drift across quarterly snapshots.
